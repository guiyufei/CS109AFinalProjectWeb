Project Overview




Background

Names are unique cultural traits that carry important information on the transformation of the social structure, whose popularity reflects the rise and fall of the social trend at certain time period. While certain names are popular in different areas in different times in history, the repetition could represent a pattern. Same names might be circulated over and over again in families! Traditionally, many cultures believe in honoring their elders and do so by naming children after them. It would be a great cultural element for us to study the social trend by focusing on the distribution of baby names. We expect to observe the duplication patterns of name distribution in the same region where population do not undergo big migration.


Individually, naming a baby is a such deeply intimate decision, which integrates with parents’ tradition, religion, favorite books and movies, their dreams for their children, and their aesthetic sensibilities in choosing names. To put it into a big picture, it is more than a personal preference, but a social artifact. For example, it has been reported that common names that were most widespread between WWI and WWII, whose prevalence has been dropping ever since, suggesting “behavioral evidence of growing individualism.” On the other hand, research has shown that a name can have a profound impact on a child that reverberates well into adulthood.


In addition, using biblical baby names to study cultural changes, especially the Christianity affiliation influence, through quantitative approaches would be able to elucidate the influence that surrounding culture wields on the parents of the newborns.


If the cultural influence affiliated to Christianity is growing or falling in the United States, there should be a corresponding increase in the cultural practices, social behaviors, and psychological processes linked to individualism. In this project, we are trying to find the evidence suggesting this is true or not. Furthermore, we are trying to find the regional influence and correlation between the states that share the similarity in culture evolution.


Project Objective

We hope to explore the social trend by analyzing the distribution of baby names. The project is divided into two parts. The first part is to analyze the biblical naming trend over years. We want to see the how the naming trend changed over different parts of the US. According to the changes, we want to explore the development of possible Christian affiliation trend. The second part is compare and explore the possible migration trend before and after some specific history events. For example, we explored how states clustered based on baby names before and after the world war II and economic depression. We are trying to explain the migration trend before and after each events according to our clustering results.

Results & Visualization


Since baby names change in a state level is a product of societal influence around certain period in the US history, analyzing the names of babies is a potential useful way to explore the major social trends as well as its impact to the overall population. Biblical name is a unique existence among all names, so we have a separate discussion for that. To explore more general social trends, we would like to use all baby names. The goal of this section is to explore whether there is a gradually change in migration pattern (or state & state relationship changing pattern) in the U.S. via the distributions of baby names from different states. Here, we assume that when people migrates, the convention of picking their baby names will not change dramatically with the location change. Therefore, we would expect a diffusion pattern between two states if there are large population movement between them. Also, if there is a migration pattern (or state & state relationship changing pattern), we would like to explore whether the change is potentially related to some major social events.

Discussion:

From the explorative analysis, we noticed that there is a change of state-state relationship over time via the distribution of baby names. Some states tend to have a closer association in the early 1990s, while they become diversified over time. This observation drives us to explore whether the change of state-state relationship pattern is due to some significant social events. We take a closer look at some major historical events happened around the pattern change time point. For example, we notice that states clusters in 1930s are different from the states clusters in 1960s. During this time period, WWII is an important historical event which may influence the migration patterns in the U.S. Thus, we compare the state-state relationship before and after WWII. By comparison, the relationship of Florida and the rest of state changes dramatically.


Also, there is a notable infusion of South states, Midwest states and West states. Before the WWII, South states (KY, NC, TN, AL, GA, FL) are well separated from the rest of surrounding states. After the WWII, those South states became more similar to states in the West (CA), Midwest (WI, IL) and North states (NY, MA, NJ). The changing pattern is consistent with the historical facts. As U.S. became involved in World War II, the West became the preferred site for many military training bases. Improved highways allowed rapid mobilization during wartime and promoted national defense during peacetime. In the Second Great Migration period (approximately from 1941 until 1970), more than five million African Americans moved to cities in states in the North, Midwest and West, including California, where Los Angeles, Oakland, Richmond, and Long Beach offered skilled jobs in the defense industry. Around 1945 the government invested approximately $70 billion in capital projects throughout California, resulting in the creation of entirely new industries and a bountiful supply of civilian jobs, which attracts more people moving from North to California. Also, World War II causes a mass migration to the shipyards and aircraft factories of San Diego (California) for soldiers and their families. This shift in population extended to the entire southern rim of the country, across the Southwest and South all the way to Georgia, Florida, and the Carolinas. Although there may be other possible reasons resulting the state-state relationship changed during this time period, WWII is surely a promising reason caused the migration, and the migration pattern is well reflected in the distribution of baby names. The possible reason of the migration pattern is well captured by baby names is that African American tends to keep their way of choosing baby names after they move to other states. Thus, there exists a similarity of baby names for those African American living in South and those moving to West, Midwest and North states.


We also explore other social events may be related to migration patterns, such as before and after Alaska was admitted as a state, and the second wave of feminism movement. The pattern of state-state relationship doesn’t change dramatically as we have expected. However, it makes sense if we explore the reason carefully. Before Alaska became a state, it is already regarded as being similar to its closest states such as WA and OR. Also, since we choose K = 3 to cluster states, although Alaska may have a larger difference from other states before it became a state, using K = 3 will not reflect such difference if it is relatively small. Furthermore, for the second wave of feminism, women might move to other industry-concentrated states for better employment opportunity, yet, they may also prefer jobs near their family, which isn’t reflected in the state migration pattern. Thus, we would expect baby names be a useful way to explore the influence of social events that involve migration and have a large social impact on certain population.

Discussion:

We are interested in examining whether biblical names could reflect Christianity influence among states over time. Although people may use biblical names even though they are not Christians, biblical names are closely associated with Christianity for the most of the time. By clustering the states using the distribution of biblical names for selected years, we notice that North-Western groups of states form well-separated clusters while North-Eastern groups of states are not well-separated over years. The social evolution of geographical correlation showing that as time goes by, the Christianity affiliation in the East coast become more diversified than the West coast. To explore the potential reason for this diversified pattern in the East coast, we took a look at two major Christianity events in the twenty century: Civil War and Separation of church and state (according to Wikipedia: History of Christianity in the United States). However, before and after those events, the pattern doesn’t show an obvious difference in state-state relationship. The possible reason is that those two events don’t have large enough (on state level) impacts on migration. Thus, we would expect the diversified results over time may due to other social events (on all names scale, including biblical names) instead of merely Christianity related events.


Conclusion

In our project, we aim to explore whether change in baby names from a state level can reflect the critical social transformations happened in US during 20th century. To examine this question, we first show the dynamics of state-state relationship over time via the distributions of baby names. Our analysis discovers that there exists a changing pattern on baby names with state-state relationships. Therefore, we further explore the association between these changes with some major social events happened around the same period in the US. We make a reasonable assumption that people tend to keep their name picking behaviors after they move to other states. Thus, if there are huge population movements during the period, we will likely observe drastic changes on baby names from state to state. Based on such hypothesis, we believe baby names could be a quantitative description of certain types of societal change.


One of the possible cultural evolutions that can be reflected by baby names change from a state level is large scale migration, huge population movements triggered by critical social events. For example, WWII causes the Second Great Migration of more than five million African Americans from the South to other states. Because of the economic concerns and huge demand for workers during war time, most of African Americans are relocated to industry-concentrated state, Urban areas and cities. For these type of events, we can employ baby names as a useful tool to capture the details of migration patterns. Moving beyond that, we can also explore the change of Christianity influence in state level over time, as well as its relationship with major Christianity events happening via the distributions of baby names. Overall, our project provides a new perspective to capture macroscopic changes in the structure of state-state relationships via baby names data. It also suggests the types of culture transformations that can be reflected and future studied by using our project results.


Yet, there exists some limitations of our project since it is based on several assumptions mentioned previously. For example, we assume that biblical names are closely related to Christianity influence. In fact, people may have a biblical name just for personal preference instead of religion purpose as being a Christian. However, from our dataset, we are not able to distinguish people with biblical names who are truly Christians by using existing predictors. Also, there may exist some unmeasurable confounders between baby names and social events. To eliminate all possible confounding issues, we need to include more detailed social information along baby names. Fortunately, the project does suggest an innovative way (using the distributions of baby names) to picture the social impact, especially migration pattern in the U.S. during 20th century.
